# Question Taxonomy

## Purpose

Visdomsatlasen is organized around human questions.

Not books.

Not religions.

Not philosophers.

Not historical periods.

Every room belongs to a human question.

Questions form a connected network that allows readers to explore humanity's shared search for wisdom.

---

# Design Principles

Questions are timeless.

A good question remains meaningful across cultures, religions and centuries.

Questions are the primary navigation of Visdomsatlasen.

Everything else exists to support them.

---

# Taxonomy Structure

The taxonomy consists of four levels.

Human Experience

↓

Themes

↓

Questions

↓

Rooms

---

# Level 1 — Human Experience

The highest level represents the broad experiences shared by all people.

Examples include:

- Living
- Suffering
- Relationships
- Meaning
- Character
- Society
- Mortality
- The Sacred

These categories are editorial tools.

They are not intended to be the primary user navigation.

---

# Level 2 — Themes

Each experience contains several themes.

Example:

Character

↓

- Courage
- Humility
- Patience
- Wisdom
- Integrity
- Gratitude
- Self-control

Themes help organize related questions.

---

# Level 3 — Questions

Questions are the heart of Visdomsatlasen.

Each question should be:

- timeless
- personal
- understandable
- open-ended

Examples:

What is courage?

Why do we suffer?

How should we forgive?

What is truth?

Why do people seek power?

What makes a good life?

How do we find peace?

---

# Level 4 — Rooms

Rooms are editorial presentations of one question.

A question may have multiple rooms.

Example:

Question

What is courage?

Rooms

- Courage in everyday life
- Courage according to the Stoics
- Courage in the Gospels
- Courage during hardship

Each room explores the same question from a different perspective.

---

---

# Sources

Every question may draw from many traditions.

Examples

Forgiveness

↓

Christianity

Stoicism

Buddhism

Judaism

Islam

Ancient Greece

Modern Psychology

History

No tradition owns the question.

Each contributes a perspective.

---

# Navigation Principles

Navigation follows questions.

Never authors.

Never books.

Never religions.

Readers explore ideas first.

Sources second.

---

# Search

Search should prioritize:

1. Questions

2. Themes

3. Rooms

4. Sources

5. Historical people

The reader should naturally arrive at questions before facts.

---

# Growth

The taxonomy should grow slowly.

Adding new questions should be uncommon.

Most new content should deepen existing questions.

A new question should only be added if it genuinely expands the map of human experience.

---

# Editorial Rules

Questions should be:

- timeless
- universal
- emotionally meaningful
- culturally broad
- easy to understand

Avoid:

- temporary issues
- political slogans
- fashionable terminology
- academic jargon

# Long-Term Vision

The taxonomy should eventually become a living map of humanity's enduring questions.

Readers should feel that they are not moving through a database.

They are travelling through a landscape of ideas.

Every room should feel like another place on that map.
